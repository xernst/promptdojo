# Launch LinkedIn post (Task 8)

For: Josh's LinkedIn, day 1 of launch (the day after the Twitter thread).
Format: long-form personal essay, first person. Normal sentence case (LinkedIn
reads better that way than all-lowercase). No em dashes. Roughly 520 words.

---

I'm 22, I just graduated, and I think the job market is about to break for
anyone who can't work with AI.

That sounds dramatic. I don't think it is. Look at what already happened, not
what might. Copywriting teams that ran 40 people now run 6. Tier-one support got
hit first and hardest. Junior design, paralegal review, first-pass analysis: all
reshaped, and most of it in the last two years. The pattern is not "AI is coming
for tech jobs." The pattern is that AI got cheap and fast at the parts of almost
every job that are structured and repeatable, and it did it everywhere at once.

Here is the part people get wrong. The skill that keeps you valuable is not
"prompting" in the influencer sense. It is the ability to point AI at real work,
read what it produces, and catch what it got wrong before it ships. That is a
real skill. It used to belong to programmers. It now belongs to everyone, and
almost nobody is being taught it.

So I built a school for it. It's called promptdojo, and it's free.

I want to be specific about what it is, because "free AI course" is a crowded
and mostly bad category. promptdojo teaches you to build by having you read code
that AI actually wrote, predict what it does, find the bug, and fix it. You do
that 833 times, in your browser, with no installs and no setup. There are ten
career paths, so a marketer learns a different route than a lawyer or an
operations manager. You are never handed a generic computer-science syllabus.
The goal is not to make you a software engineer. The goal is to make you the
person who can direct AI instead of being replaced by it.

It is free for individuals, and it is staying that way. There is no paywall and
no "free preview" that runs out. The way it stays free is that companies pay to
train their teams on it. Corporate training funds the public version. I would
rather have that model than charge the exact people who are most exposed to all
of this.

That last part is the real reason I built it. I think the next five years are
going to be hard for a lot of people whose work is changing under them faster
than they can react. Putting a paywall on the way out of that felt wrong. So I
didn't.

If you are reading this and your job feels different than it did a year ago:
this is for you. Go try it. It takes about two minutes to start.

If you run a team that is going to have to reskill at scale, and most teams are:
this is also for you, and I would genuinely like to talk. Reach out about
training your people on it.

And if it is not for you but you know someone it is for, the most useful thing
you can do is send it to them.
